00 ‚Äî Overview

Payment app to
digital lifestyle platform

PayPay launched October 2018 with one purpose: make QR-code payments easy in Japan. By February 2026, it handles payments, insurance, investing, banking ‚Äî with 72 million users and a landmark US IPO filing. The design team had to evolve the interface every step without losing those users.

BREAKING ‚Äî February 12, 2026: PayPay Files US IPO on Nasdaq

PayPay Corporation filed its F-1 prospectus with the SEC for listing on the Nasdaq Global Select Market under ticker symbol "PAYP." The filing revealed 72 million registered users as of December 31, 2025 ‚Äî representing 75% penetration among Japan's 96 million smartphone users. Estimated IPO valuation: ~$2 billion. 01 ‚Äî THE CHALLENGE

A Home Screen at Breaking Point

Mini apps, loyalty programs, insurance, and fintech services were colliding on a single screen. Feature growth made the product less usable ‚Äî discoverability collapsed, and users stopped scrolling to find new capabilities.

02 ‚Äî THE APPROACH

Design-Led Redesign + System

A user-first, research-grounded redesign led ‚Äî unusually ‚Äî by the Design Team rather than a PM. Simultaneously, a company-wide Style Guide was built from scratch to ensure quality at scale across 14+ designers from 10+ countries.

03 ‚Äî THE RESULT

72M Users, US IPO, Global Ambition

V3.0 in 2021 was the design inflection point. By 2025 PayPay had 72 million users, 7.46 billion annual transactions ‚Äî 20% of all cashless payments in Japan ‚Äî and a Nasdaq IPO filing. The design system built in 2021 is the foundation of that scale.

03 ‚Äî Design Challenges

Four problems that
forced the redesign

Before V3.0, the design team audited V2.x and identified four critical UX failures ‚Äî supported by behavioral data, not just intuition. These problems, if unsolved, would have capped PayPay's growth well before 72 million users.

01
üó∫Ô∏è

Mini App Discoverability Crisis

As PayPay added mini apps rapidly ‚Äî Mobile Charge, PCR Test Kits, insurance products, investments ‚Äî the growing icon grid became impossible to navigate. Users couldn't find new services. Partner integrations were being built and effectively lost. This directly threatened PayPay's super app ambitions.

‚Üí Restructured 4-column grid with priority ordering above fold
02
üìú

Scroll Engagement Drop-off

User behavior data: users were not scrolling below the visible fold. Critical information positioned lower was effectively unseen. The longer the screen grew with each release, the worse this became ‚Äî a compounding failure invisible to the team until data surfaced it. This wasted enormous investment in features nobody found.

‚Üí Content density rebalancing, visual hierarchy restructure
03
üì£

Campaign Visibility Failure

PayPay's massive campaigns ‚Äî the engine of its growth ‚Äî were buried in banners at the bottom. The "10 Billion Yen Giveaway Campaign" proved what was possible when users saw promotions. Every subsequent campaign was running at reduced effectiveness due to a design architecture that hid it.

‚Üí Above-the-fold promotional real estate
04
üß©

Design Inconsistency at Scale

Five designers in 2018, fourteen by 2021, from 10+ countries. No shared rules meant different aesthetics across features. As mini apps multiplied and third-party partners integrated their services, consistency became impossible without a formal system. This threatened PayPay's brand as it approached the scale of national infrastructure.

‚Üí The PayPay Style Guide ‚Äî built parallel to weekly releases
04 ‚Äî Design Principles

The philosophy behind
PayPay's design system

When Maki Aoyagi (YAGI-san) led creation of the PayPay Style Guide, she distilled the design philosophy into six principles guiding every decision ‚Äî from icon sizes to campaign layouts to research culture. These principles are what enabled 72 million users.

PRINCIPLE 01

Payment First, Always

However many features PayPay adds, barcode/QR payment must remain instantly accessible. A user approaching checkout should never hunt. This is a trust requirement ‚Äî not a design constraint. It's why PayPay survived while 7Pay and others failed.

PRINCIPLE 02

Consistent Across the Ecosystem

The Style Guide applies to PayPay, PayPay Bank, PayPay Securities, PayPay Card, and all partner mini apps. Users moving between services feel continuity. Consistency reduces cognitive load and increases perceived reliability ‚Äî critical when you're processing ¬•15.39 trillion in annual GMV.

PRINCIPLE 03

No Unpleasant Surprises

Payments are high-stakes moments. Design changes must never create confusion during a transaction. Gradual rollouts, A/B testing, and careful change management are non-negotiable. Good design that creates a bad experience is not good design ‚Äî and in fintech, a bad experience loses a user permanently.

PRINCIPLE 04

Users First ¬∑ Speed Second

Two values coexist: Users First (is this worthwhile? listen, analyze data, research, iterate) and Speed (deliver ASAP, trial-and-error over perfection, weekly improvement cycles). PayPay updates every week without exception. This cadence is only possible because the Style Guide makes quality systematic.

PRINCIPLE 05

Design System as Velocity Infrastructure

The Style Guide enables speed without sacrificing quality. 14+ designers can build new features weekly without relitigating fundamental decisions every sprint. At 72 million users in 2025, the design system that seemed unnecessary at 5 designers is now the foundation of national financial infrastructure.

PRINCIPLE 06

"PayPayness" ‚Äî Brand Distinctiveness

The long-term vision includes a PayPay-original typeface. Every design decision cultivates a distinctive brand character ‚Äî a visual language as distinctive as PayPay's red. With an IPO on Nasdaq as ticker "PAYP," this brand identity now has global significance.

08 ‚Äî Design Process

How V3.0
actually happened

Design led this project ‚Äî not PM. Hong Du, 4 months into his tenure, spearheaded design. Sunna Mo handled internal coordination across 20+ engineering teams. This is the process that built the foundation for 72 million users.

1

Problem Audit

Four data-backed UX failures identified. Problems defined before solutions explored. User research team embedded in design.

2

Constraint Mapping

Hong mapped all existing specs, undocumented constraints, and cross-team dependencies while simultaneously learning the company structure.

3

Concept Iteration

Multiple IA and layout directions explored. Style Guide provided guardrails. Team workshops. Engineering consulted on feasibility each cycle.

4

Stakeholder Alignment

Every engineering team received specs. Sunna coordinated all departments. Trust built through transparent progress sharing with Product leadership.

5

Launch + Scale

June 2021 launch. Twitter praise confirmed direction. This V3.0 design foundation then scaled PayPay from 42M to 72M users by December 2025.

Building Design Culture at Startup Speed ‚Äî That Scaled to an IPO

PayPay launched three months after founding. There were no design rules ‚Äî five designers, five aesthetics. Maki Aoyagi recognized this worked at 5 but would fail at 14. She built the Style Guide while shipping weekly updates, building the runway while the plane was flying.

Hong Du was handed one of PayPay's most visible projects within weeks of joining. He simultaneously learned the company, mapped undocumented specifications, and handled inquiries from practically every engineer. The output ‚Äî V3.0 ‚Äî earned widespread Twitter praise the day it launched.

Fast-forward to 2026: that same design system is now the foundation of a company serving 72 million users ‚Äî 75% of Japan's smartphone users ‚Äî and filing a ~$2 billion IPO on Nasdaq. The design decisions made in 2021 directly enabled that scale.

PayPay's culture of flat hierarchy, global diversity (35+ countries), and design ownership ‚Äî giving designers decision-making authority rather than just Figma files ‚Äî is what made this possible.

11 ‚Äî Key Lessons

What PayPay's journey
teaches product designers

Four lessons ‚Äî now validated by 72 million users, 7.46 billion annual transactions, and a US IPO filing in February 2026.

01

Design systems are growth infrastructure

PayPay went from 5 to 14+ designers across 10+ nationalities. Without the Style Guide, quality degrades as headcount scales.

The Style Guide built in 2021 is now the foundation of a company processing ¬•15.39 trillion in annual GMV and filing a $2B Nasdaq IPO. At 5 designers it felt unnecessary. At 14, essential. At 72M users, it's national-scale infrastructure. Build your design system before you think you need it.

02

Payment UX demands continuity over novelty

Changing a payment interface creates user anxiety ‚Äî especially establishing trust in digital payments in a market still adapting to cashless behavior.

PayPay is now designated by Japan's Financial Services Agency as a Specified Essential Infrastructure Service Provider. The trust built through consistent, non-surprising UX decisions over 7 years is what earned that designation ‚Äî and the 40 million monthly active users who transact on it every month.

03

Feature growth ‚â† screen growth

V2 tried to surface everything on one screen. The result: nothing was discoverable. More features made the app less useful.

PayPay now handles payments, banking, investing, insurance, remittances, and shopping ‚Äî all from one app. This is only possible because V3.0 established an IA that could scale. Structural audits and priority-based information architecture ‚Äî not just visual polish ‚Äî are what made PayPay a true super app at scale.

04

Design leadership requires organizational trust

V3.0 was led by Design, not PM ‚Äî unusual, requiring leadership to trust a designer 4 months into their tenure with a project affecting 42 million users.

PayPay's willingness to give designers decision-making authority ‚Äî not just Figma files ‚Äî created an environment where design quality compounded over 7 years into a product now seeking a $2 billion IPO. The flat hierarchy and global culture that enabled this is a competitive advantage as real as any technology.
